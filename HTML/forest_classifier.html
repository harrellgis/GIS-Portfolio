Developing a Tree Canopy Classification Model Using the 2016 National Agricultural Aerial Imagery Program (NAIP)

background

Even with the proliferation of modern remote sensing platforms, there remains a constant tradeoff between data coverage and data accuracy. While many lower resolution platforms provide global coverage and high revisit rates, their data products are often not appropriate for regional or site-specific analysis.

Some of the unresolved issues with the use of low/medium resolution products such as SPOT (20m), Landsat (30m), or MODIS (250m) are their inability to model small-scale features, and the limits of their application for highly heterogeneous landscapes. As can be seen in figure 1, the course resolution of MODIS or Landsat pixels may not be able to capture the complexity of mixed land covers. The statistical methods used by these platforms to classify land cover (i.e. majority land cover) may result in large amounts of data loss.

The National Agricultural Imagery Program (NAIP) provides high-resolution 4-band aerial imagery, with a ground sample distance of 1 meter for the conterminous United States. Since 2009 this program has provided national coverage every three years, which makes it a highly accurate and reliable environmental monitoring tool.

The North Carolina Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (NCAG&CS) uses this imagery for a variety of applications including emergency services, agricultural, and forest resource monitoring. Using NAIP imagery, this project will provide the NCAG&CS with a improved tree canopy classification method which can aid in land management practices such as timber harvesting, restoration, fire management and other critical activities.

problem

In 2016, the NC Agricultural Dept. hired a GIS contractor to develop a data product known as the 2016 Tree Canopy Project. The goal of this project was to use NAIP imagery to extract tree canopy cover at 1m resolution across the state of North Carolina.

Problem areas within the dataset:

  • Shadows cast by topography
  • Mixed forests
  • Agricultural lands

The null data likely comes from dark and/or heterogeneous landcover, making it hard to distinguish between class spectral values.

The goal of this project was to create an improved semi-automated supervised tree canopy classification methodology in order to provide a higher quality data product than the one produced by the 2016 NAIP Tree Canopy Project. The final data products were displayed in a web application with swipe functionality to allow users to compare between the 2016 and improved 2018 tree canopy classification models.

considerations & requirements

  • 600GB of raw NAIP imagery for the State of North Carolina
  • Approximately 3,564 8-bit 4 band images
  • Required to use NC State VCL (Virtual Computing Library) Infrastructure with a storage capacity of 100GB
  • Imagery must be transferred over the web
  • The classification process is semi-automated, meaning tasks such as collecting training polygons must be done manually
  • Random Trees Algorithm
    • Computationally Efficient
    • Resistant to over fitting
    • Ability to incorporate non-spectral information

image classification methods

  1. RGB, NIR, and Derived Texture Variables (StDev, GLCM)
  2. Single-Vector Decomposition Principal Component Analysis
  3. Resample top 6 Principal Components
  4. Create training polygons
  5. Run Random Trees Classifier
  6. Post-ClassificationProcessing
    1. Focal majority filter
    2. Smoothing and clumping
    3. Generalize class boundaries

discussion

The results produced by the 2018 classification demonstrate the strengths of the Random Trees classification algorithm. Specifically, the algorithm's resistance to overfitting allowed it to create a much higher accuracy classification. As can be seen in the Bladen Lakes example (Figure 6), the 2016 model resulted in the over-classification of tree canopy areas. This is especially apparent in areas of forest at different stages of development as well as in areas that had large amounts of vegetation, but did not contain mature tree canopies. In the DuPont State Forest example (Figure 6), the 2018 model greatly improved the classification accuracy of mixed forests and areas containing shadows cast by the mountainous topography.

These initial results also reveal useful patterns for the further development of the model. Specifically, as can be seen in the Bladen Lakes results, the land cover classes that are most often misclassified include grass and cultivated areas. This is likely due to the similarities in the spectral characteristics of these classes to forested land cover. Further refinement of training samples as well as the possible inclusion of alternate texture variables could further increase the accuracy of the model.

  • This workflow is particularly applicable to site-specific analysis
  • This method would need to be streamlined (i.e. single software) to improve scalability
  • Accuracy assessment would be beneficial to make results more empirical

conclusion

This method of image classification demonstrates a 'proof of concept' for efficiently producing high accuracy land classification models. Further development of these applications would provide a valuable land management tool for tracking multi-year land use change. This method could be applied to other land-cover types and use cases. For example, defining crop areas, flood inundation, land-use classifications.

Finally, the main limiting factor for utilizing this classification method is computational power. The incorporation of parallel processing and high performance computing (HPC) would drastically decrease the processing time required by this methodology. One promising avenue for the further development of this model is the Google Earth Engine platform. Not only does Earth Engine give users access to the entire catalog of NAIP imagery for North Carolina, its HPC architecture allows for complex geo-processing to be conducted at large spatial scales more efficiently. Developing a model compatible with Google Earth Engine would allow multiyear analysis of NAIP imagery. An increase in the temporal scale of this project would provide interesting insights into the dynamics of forest growth and could provide valuable information for timber harvesting and restoration activities.
