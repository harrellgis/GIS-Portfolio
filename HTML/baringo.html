shaded reliefs

A common task among GIS users is to create shaded relief or hillshade maps. A shaded relief is a 3D representation of the earths surface which is used to enhance features with large topographic variation such as mountains, valleys, or plateaus. GIS software calculates shaded reliefs by illuminating an elevation surface with a point light source, mimicking sun altitude and azimuth angle. Shaded relief maps are often used as base layers and combined with semi-transparent thematic layers in order to enhance the data with topographic information.

Although standard GIS software packages have the ability to create hillshades, the results are often noisy and harsh. Furthermore, because GIS software centers primarily on 2D rather than 3D representations of data, users have limited control over the parameters used to create hillshades. However, a new approach for creating realistic shaded relief maps is being popularized by cartographers, and that approach utilizes Blender3D. Blender is an extremely powerful open-source software specifically designed for 3D modelling. Blender has the ability to simulate the complexities of light scattering, reflectance, and shadows in a 3D environment in a way that GIS software does not. The results are are far superior and more realistic looking hillshades. For a great tutorial on the basics of creating shaded reliefs in Blender, click here.

digital map refurbishment

The purpose of this project was to explore the process of creating shaded reliefs in Blender. After developing a sufficient workflow I decided to undertake the refurbishment of a series of four 1980's vintage maps of Kenya created by the Kenya Soil Survey. The digital map images came from photographs of hard-copy maps which had various damaged areas including discoloration, folds, and tears. I first addressed the damaged areas by color correcting the main map image with photo editing software before cropping it to a new background. Next, vector editing software was used to rebuild all map elements including titles, legends, map keys, and lables from scratch. Specific attention was paid to improving the overall readability of the map. This was accomplished by increasing font sizes, grouping map keys and legends, and repositioning elements to increase whitespace.

Because the original map images did not contain spatial reference information, they had to be georeferenced in QGIS3. This allowed me to extract the map's bounding box, which was used to obtain 30m SRTM elevation data in Google Earth Engine. Finally, the refurbished map Images and elevation data were combined in Blender to create an orthographic shaded relief map.
