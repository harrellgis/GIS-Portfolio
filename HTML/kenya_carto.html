applying modern cartography to vintage maps of kenya

software tools:

A common task among GIS users is to create shaded relief or hillshade maps. A shaded relief is a 3D representation of the earths surface which is used to enhance features with large topographic variation such as mountains, valleys, or plateaus. GIS software calculates shaded reliefs by illuminating a three dimensional surface with a point light source, mimicking sun elevation and azimuth angle. Shaded relief maps are often used as base elevation layers and combined with semi-transparent thematic layers in order to enhance the data with topographic information.

Although standard GIS software packages have the ability to create hillshades, the results are often noisy and harsh. Furthermore, because GIS software centers primarily on 2D rather than 3D representations of data (although this paradigm is shifting) users have limited control over the parameters used to calculate hillshades. However, a new approach to creating realistic shaded relief maps is being embraced by cartographers, and that approach is Blender3D. Blender is an extremely powerful open-source software specifically designed for 3D modelling. Blender is able to simulate the complexities of light scattering, reflectance, and shadows in a way that conventional GIS software cannot. The result is a far superior and more realistic looking hillshade. For a great tutorial on the basics of creating shaded reliefs in Blender, click here.

The purpose of this project was to explore the process of creating shaded reliefs in Blender. After developing a sufficient workflow I decided to undertake a full refurbishment of a series of four 1980's vintage maps of Kenya created by the Kenya Soil Survey. The digital map files came from photographs of paper maps which had various damaged areas including discoloration, folds, ink writing, and tears. After color correcting the original maps I used vector editing softwares to rebuild all map elements including titles, map keys, and lables from scratch. Once the maps were georeferencing using QGIS, I was able to extract the map's bounding box, which was used to obtain 30m SRTM elevation data in Google Earth Engine. Finally, the refurbished map Images and elevation data were combined in Blender to create an orthographic shaded relief map.
